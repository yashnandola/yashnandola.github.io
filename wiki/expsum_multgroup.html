Exponential sums on multiplicative groups

Contents

  • References

If \(G \subseteq \mathbb{F}_P^{*}\) is a small multiplicative subgroup of \(\mathbb{F}_p^{*}\), it is likely that \(G\), and all the cosets \({xG}_{x \in \mathbb{F}_p^{*}}\) are going to be well-distributed in \(\mathbb{F}_p\). This is the theme of the sum-product phenomenon in number theory.

Fix the notation \(e_{p}(x) = e^{\frac{2\pi i}{p} x}\). We state the following theorem, as given in [1].

There exist \(C_1,C_2>0\) such that whenever \(G \subseteq \mathbb{F}_p^{*}\) is a finite group such that \(|G| \ge p^{\delta}\), we have \[\begin{align} \max_{x \in \mathbb{F}_p^{*} } \left| \frac{1}{|G|} \sum_{y \in G}^{} e_{p}(xy)\right| \le p^{-\varepsilon}, \end{align}\] where \(\varepsilon = \exp \left( {\frac{-C_1}{\delta^{C_2}}} \right)\).

  1. The numbers \(C_1,C_2\) are independent of \(p\).
  2. Note that the LHS is trivially \(\le 1\). The bound given above becomes closer to this trivial bound more and more as \(\delta \rightarrow 0\) because of the nature of dependence of \(\varepsilon\) on \(\delta\).

So this theorem basically tells us that when \(G \subseteq \mathbb{F}_p^{*}\) is a subgroup, arithmetic progressions inside \(G\) become highly unlikely.
